Binary phase-shift keying (BPSK), in which the carrier amplitude and carrier frequency are both maintained constant, while the carrier phase is keyed between the two possible values (e.g., 0° and 180°) used to represent symbols 0 and 1.

GENERATION AND COHERENT DETECTION OF BPSK SIGNALS

Generation

BPSK signal is a special case of DSB- SC modulation. Specifically, we use a product modulator consisting of two components (see Fig):

1. Non-return-to-zero level encoder, whereby the input binary data sequence is encoded in polar form with symbols 1 and 0 represented by the constant-amplitude levels: √Eb and -√Eb, respectively.

2. Product modulator, which multiplies the level-encoded binary wave by the sinusoidal carrier c(t)of amplitude √2/Tb to produce the BPSK signal

Detection
  • To detect the original binary sequence of 1s and 0s, the BPSK signal at the channel output is applied to a receiver that consists of four sections
  • Product modulator, which is also supplied with a locally generated reference signal that is a replica of the carrier wave c(t).
  • Low-pass filter, designed to remove the double- frequency components of the product modulator output (i.e., the components centered on 2fc and pass the zero-frequency components.
  • Sampler, which uniformly samples the output of the low-pass filter at t=iTb where i=0,±1; ±2....; the local clock governing the operation of the sampler is synchronized with the clock responsible for bit-timing in the transmitter.
  • Decision-making device, which compares the sampled value of the low-pass filter's output to an externally supplied
  • threshold. If the threshold is exceeded, the device decides in favor of symbol 1; otherwise, it decides in favor of symbol 0.

The BPSK receiver described in Fig. is said to be coherent in the sense that the sinusoidal reference signal applied to the product modulator in the demodulator is synchronous in phase (and, of course, frequency) with the carrier wave used in the modulator.This requirement can be achieved by using a phase-locked loop.

Binary data stream:

A binary data stream is a sequence of digital bits (0s and 1s) that represents information in a digital form. This information could be anything from text, audio, images, or any other digital content. In the context of digital communication systems, a binary data stream is typically used as the input signal to be transmitted over a communication channel.

Carrier wave:

A carrier wave is a high-frequency sinusoidal waveform that is used to transmit the binary data stream over a communication channel. The carrier wave has a fixed frequency and amplitude, and its sole purpose is to modulate the binary data stream. The carrier wave is typically generated by an oscillator or a waveform generator, and its frequency is chosen to be higher than the highest frequency of the binary data stream to be transmitted.

Phase-shift keying:

Phase-shift keying (PSK) is a digital modulation technique that uses the phase of the carrier wave to transmit digital data. In PSK, the phase of the carrier wave is varied to represent the binary data stream. For example, a binary 0 could be represented by a carrier wave with a phase of 0 degrees, while a binary 1 could be represented by a carrier wave with a phase of 180 degrees.

To transmit a binary data stream using PSK, the binary data stream is first encoded into a series of digital bits, which are then used to modulate the phase of the carrier wave. At the receiver end, the modulated carrier wave is demodulated to recover the original binary data stream. PSK is a more efficient modulation technique compared to ASK since it requires less bandwidth to transmit the same amount of data. However, it is more complex to implement and is also susceptible to noise and interference.
